# Case Study: The Anatomy of a Phishing Attack

### **What the Crime Is**
**Phishing** is a type of social engineering cybercrime where an attacker poses as a legitimate institution—such as a bank, a government agency, or a popular service like Netflix—to trick individuals into providing sensitive data. This data typically includes personally identifiable information (PII), banking and credit card details, or account passwords.

### **How It Typically Happens (Step-by-Step)**
1.  **The Bait:** The attacker sends a high-urgency email or SMS (Smishing) that mimics a trusted brand’s official communication.
2.  **The Hook:** The message creates a sense of "artificial crisis," such as "Your account will be suspended in 2 hours" or "Suspicious activity detected on your UPI ID."
3.  **The Redirect:** The user is pressured to click a link that leads to a "spoofed" website. This site is designed to look identical to a real login page (e.g., a fake bank portal).
4.  **The Harvest:** Once the victim enters their credentials or OTP (One-Time Password) on the fake site, the attacker captures the data in real-time.
5.  **The Payoff:** The attacker immediately uses the stolen info to drain bank accounts, make unauthorized purchases, or sell the data on the dark web.

### **Target Audience**
While attackers use "spray and pray" tactics to target the general public, they often focus on **students** (via fake scholarship or fee payment links) and **senior citizens**, who may be less familiar with verifying digital certificates or URL structures.

### **Consequences**
The consequences are often devastating, ranging from **immediate financial loss** and identity theft to long-term credit score damage. For students, a successful phishing attack can lead to the loss of academic data or unauthorized access to university portals, resulting in significant emotional stress and a breach of personal privacy.
